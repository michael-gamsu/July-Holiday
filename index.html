Why this route works

  • Schengen onlyBoth Italy and Croatia are Schengen — one visa covers the whole trip. No UK visa needed.
  • Rome firstArrive fresh for the city sightseeing, then wind down on the Adriatic coast and islands for the rest of the trip.
  • Easy connectionsRome to Dubrovnik is a short ~1.5h flight. Dubrovnik to Hvar is reachable by catamaran or via a short ferry hop.
  • Budget-friendly CroatiaDubrovnik and Hvar offer stunning scenery, great food, and excellent value — especially eating at local konobas away from the main tourist strips.

Action checklist — do these now

  • Book JNB–Rome flightsJuly is peak — look for direct or one-stop options via Ethiopian, Emirates, or Qatar Airways. Book immediately.
  • Book Rome–Dubrovnik flightBudget airlines like Vueling, EasyJet or Croatia Airlines. Short flight, book early for best prices.
  • Apply for Schengen visaItaly or Croatia as main destination. €80 per person via VFS Global in Johannesburg. Allow 6–8 weeks.
  • Book Rome accommodation4 nights near the historic centre — Trastevere or Monti neighbourhoods offer the best mix of location and value.
  • Book Croatia accommodationJuly is peak season — Dubrovnik Old Town apartments and Hvar accommodation sell out. Book 3–4 months ahead.
  • Get travel insuranceRequired for Schengen visa. Must cover €30,000 medical minimum. Get this before applying for visas.

Rome — Complete Guide

4 nights · Tue 1 – Sat 5 July · Staying in Monti

Getting to Monti from the airport

  • Taxi (recommended night 1)Fixed rate €55 for up to 4 passengers to any address inside the Aurelian Walls, including Monti. Door to door. Take only official white taxis from the rank outside arrivals — never touts inside the terminal. If a driver quotes more, show them the legally fixed fare.
  • Train (budget option)Leonardo Express from Fiumicino to Roma Termini, every 15 mins, 32 min journey, €14pp. Last service 11:38pm. From Termini, Monti is a 10-minute walk or 5-minute taxi. Total ~€15pp but more effort with luggage late at night.

Day 1 — July 1: Arrive + First taste of Monti

  • ~11pm arrivalDrop bags. Walk 2 minutes to Piazza della Madonna dei Monti — the beating heart of your neighbourhood. Buy wine from Edicola Wine Bar, sit on the fountain steps with locals. This is Rome at its most effortless. Bed by 1am.

Day 2 — July 2: Ancient Rome + Testaccio

  • 8:30am — Colosseum10-minute walk from your hotel. Go first thing before the heat builds. Without pre-booked tickets in July you will queue 2+ hours — book now at coopculture.it, ~€18pp. Allow 2.5–3 hours for Colosseum + Roman Forum.
  • 12pm — SiestaIt will be 35°C. Non-negotiable. Shower, recharge, air-con. The Romans invented this for a reason.
  • 3:30pm — TestaccioLate lunch at Mercato Testaccio — Mordi e Vai stall inside the market. Roman sandwiches with braised meats, ~€5–7. Unfussy and brilliant.
  • 5pm — Aventine HillFree walk up through the orange garden (Giardino degli Aranci) — panoramic views over Rome. Then the Knights of Malta keyhole on Piazza dei Cavalieri di Malta — peer through the keyhole and see St Peter's dome perfectly framed at the end of a hedge-lined avenue. One of Rome's best free moments.
  • 8pm — AperitivoAi Tre Scalini, Via Panisperna 251. Tiny, packed with locals, exceptional house wine and snacks. No booking needed.
  • 9:30pm — DinnerFlavio al Velavevodetto, Via di Monte Testaccio 97, Testaccio. Built into an ancient mound of broken Roman amphorae — spectacular setting, outstanding Roman food. Book via TheFork or their website. ~€20–25pp.

Day 3 — July 3: Jewish Quarter + Trastevere + Trevi at Midnight

  • 9am — Pasticceria BoccioneVia del Portico d'Ottavia 1. The oldest surviving bakery in Rome's Jewish Ghetto, established 1815. No sign outside, tiny shop, grumpy staff, cash only — and completely unmissable. Go at opening before the ricotta tart sells out. Get: Ricotta e visciole (ricotta and sour cherry tart, ~€4–6 a slice) and Pizza ebraica (sweet bread with almonds, candied ginger, pine nuts, raisins). Eat standing on the cobblestones outside.
  • 9:30am — Jewish QuarterCompact and beautiful — allow 1.5–2 hours: Portico d'Ottavia (ancient Roman arch that doubled as the ghetto gate), Piazza delle Cinque Scole, Great Synagogue exterior, Jewish Museum (~€12pp, bring ID), Ponte Fabricio (Rome's oldest bridge, built 62 BC) — cross to Tiber Island for views. Dress modestly if entering the synagogue.
  • 1pm — Nonna Betta ⭐Via del Portico d'Ottavia 16. Authentic Roman-Jewish cuisine — order: carciofi alla giudia (whole deep-fried artichoke, mandatory), fiori di zucca (courgette flowers stuffed with ricotta and anchovy), whatever pasta is on the daily menu. ~€20–25pp with wine. Book now at nonnabetta.it — essential for July. Backup: Ba'Ghetto next door (baghetto.com).
  • 3pm — SiestaYou've walked a lot and eaten well. Rest.
  • 6pm — Campo de' FioriThe market is over but the square is lively in the evening. Walk through, then 10 minutes to Piazza Navona.
  • 7:30pm — Da Enzo al 29Via dei Vascellari 29, Trastevere. Intimate, just a few tables. The carbonara and amatriciana are benchmark versions. Book now at daenzoal29.it or TheFork — tiny restaurant. ~€20–25pp.
  • 9:30pm — Piazza Trilussa 🎶Two minutes from Da Enzo. The heart of Trastevere nightlife. Every summer evening from ~9pm, street musicians set up at the fountain and the whole square ends up singing and dancing. The song is "Sarà Perché Ti Amo" — you'll know it within 30 seconds, and you'll be singing within 10 minutes. Buy cheap wine from a minimarket (~€1.50), find a spot on the fountain steps, and let it happen.
  • 11:30pm — Trevi Fountain 🌙Walk or short taxi (~15 mins). At midnight the marble glows golden under the lights, the water sounds loud in the quiet streets, and the crowds are a fraction of daytime. Stand at the edge. Toss your coin. Give it 30–45 minutes. Walk home through empty lamplit streets. Bed ~1:30am. Worth every minute.

Day 4 — July 4: Vatican + Hostaria Dino e Tony

  • 8:30am — Vatican MuseumsBook immediately at museivaticani.va — sells out weeks ahead in July. ~€17–20pp. Go first thing. Allow at least 3 hours — the Sistine Chapel alone justifies the visit.
  • 12pm — St Peter's BasilicaFree entry. The dome climb is ~€8 and gives one of the great views over Rome. Do it before midday heat peaks.
  • 1:30pm — Lunch in PratiWalk 5 minutes north of the Vatican into residential Prati. Any alimentari (deli) for a made-to-order sandwich ~€4–6. Avoid anything facing St Peter's Square — pure tourist trap pricing.
  • 3pm — Siesta
  • 5:30pm — Centro StoricoPantheon (~€5 entry), then Piazza Navona.
  • 7:30pm — Dino e Tony ⭐⭐Hostaria Dino e Tony, Via Leone IV 60, Prati — 5-minute walk from the Vatican. 4.4 stars, 3,178 reviews. Price level 1. There is no menu. Dino comes to the table, speaks Italian, and starts bringing food. Just say "sì" to everything. Antipasti arrive in waves — salumi, supplì, fried artichokes, focaccia — then pasta, then dessert, then limoncello you didn't order. The rigatoni alla gricia has a devoted following. ~€20–25pp including wine. To book: call +39 06 3973 3284. Say your name, "due persone", and the date. Closed Sundays — July 4 is Friday ✓
  • ~10pm — Monti nightcapWalk back through Centro Storico. Edicola Wine Bar on Piazza della Madonna dei Monti — converted from an old newspaper kiosk, excellent wine by the glass. Your local square.

Day 5 — July 5: Slow morning + Borghese Gallery + Depart

  • Morning — MontiSleep in. Saturday street market in Piazza della Madonna dei Monti with jewellery, leather goods, vintage prints and ceramics. Walk Via del Boschetto and Via Panisperna — independent coffee bars, vintage shops, antique dealers.
  • 11am — GelatoGelateria del Teatro, Via dei Coronari 65 — considered one of the best in Rome. 20-minute walk through beautiful streets.
  • 1pm — Mercato CentraleInside Roma Termini station, level 1. Sounds unglamorous — it genuinely isn't. Multiple stalls by serious Roman food producers. Pasta, pizza, porchetta, supplì. ~€10–12.
  • 2:30pm — Borghese Gallery ⭐Book at galleriaborghese.it — ~€15pp. Strictly timed entry, max 360 people, so it never feels crowded. Bernini's sculptures in person — Daphne transforming into a tree, Apollo's fingers pressing into marble that looks like flesh — are genuinely breathtaking. Allow 2 hours.
  • 5pm — Siesta + pack
  • 7pm — Final dinnerKeep it simple. A neighbourhood trattoria in Monti, a glass of Frascati, no rush.
  • 9pm — Leonardo ExpressTrain from Roma Termini to Fiumicino, 32 min, ~€14pp. Comfortable for check-in ahead of 11pm departure to Dubrovnik.

Key tips

  • Siesta is real1–4pm in July Rome is brutal. Going back to the hotel mid-afternoon means you actually enjoy the evenings rather than wilting through them.
  • Trevi at midnightLit up gold, far fewer people, you can hear the water. Give it 30–45 minutes.
  • Piazza TrilussaThe singing is driven by buskers so not guaranteed every single night, but in peak July it happens regularly. Go regardless — the piazza is fantastic either way.
  • Dino e TonyJust say sì. Don't fight it. Trust Dino.
  • BoccioneGo at opening, cash only, no menu, potentially grumpy staff. Get the ricotta tart. It's extraordinary.
  • WaterRome has free drinking fountains (nasoni) everywhere. Bring a refillable bottle. July heat is serious.
  • WalkingMonti puts you within 10–15 minutes of almost everything on this itinerary. You barely need public transport.

Croatia — Activities & Eating Guide

14 nights · Dubrovnik (July 6–12) + Hvar (July 12–19)

Dubrovnik — July 6–12 · Activities

City Walls Walk ~€35pp / R690pp
The one non-negotiable in Dubrovnik. Go at 8am before heat and cruise ship crowds. One-way loop, ~2km, takes 1.5–2 hours. Views over Old Town and Adriatic are genuinely spectacular.
Old Town wander Free
Stradun, the side streets, Gundulić Square market in the morning. Just walk and get lost.
Buža Bar & Mala Buža ~€7 a beer
Famous cliff bars on the south wall — hole-in-the-wall literally carved into the city walls, drinks served above the sea. Jump off the rocks below. Go late afternoon — the light is magical. Pricey but the experience is worth one round.
Lokrum Island ~€30 return inc. park
Short ferry from Old Port. Pine forest, peacocks, saltwater lake for swimming, ruins. Great for a full lazy day. Pack a picnic to keep costs down.
Banje Beach Free
Closest beach to Old Town, 5 min from Ploče Gate. Views back over the walls. Gets busy by 10am — arrive early or go late afternoon.
Šulić Beach Free
Quieter, near Fort Lovrijenac. More local feel than Banje.
Hike Mount Srđ Free
45 mins up, extraordinary panoramic views. Go early morning only — in July midday heat it's brutal.
Sunset sea kayaking ~€40pp / R800pp
Kayak tour around the walls at sunset. Excellent if you want one active experience. Book on GetYourGuide a week or two before.
  • Day trip: CavtatBus 10 from bus station, €3 each way, 30 mins. Beautiful quiet coastal village, palm-lined promenade, good swimming. Much cheaper and more relaxed than Dubrovnik. Perfect for one slow afternoon.

Where to eat in Dubrovnik

The rule: never eat on Stradun or facing the walls. Walk one or two streets back and prices drop significantly.

Kamenice Lunch · €€
Gundulić Square. Famous for fried fish, mussels, seafood risotto. Outdoor tables on the market square, very local crowd, excellent value. No booking needed, just queue.
Taj Mahal Dinner · ~€10–15pp
Nikole Gučetića 2. Bosnian food — burek, ćevapi, lamb dishes. Sounds unexpected but it's brilliant, cheap, and completely non-touristy. A local favourite.
Lucin Kantun Dinner · €€
Od Sigurate 1, just off Stradun. Proper Dalmatian food, fish, grilled meats. Locals eat here. Book ahead in July.
Proto Special dinner · ~€25–30pp
Široka 1. Genuinely excellent seafood, open since 1886. Worth one special dinner.
  • ĆevapiFrom any local grill — minced meat sausages in flatbread with onions and ajvar sauce. €4–6, filling, delicious.
  • BurekFrom the bakery near Pile Gate end of Stradun — flaky pastry with cheese or meat. €2–3 lunch on the go.
  • Green MarketGundulić Square mornings — fresh produce, local cheese, olives. Great for self-catering breakfast supplies.
  • Cheap drinksCold Karlovačko or Ožujsko beer from a supermarket (~€1.50) and find a bench or beach spot. Far better value than bar prices, perfectly acceptable in Croatia.

Hvar — July 12–19 · Activities

Pakleni Islands Water taxi ~€5–10 each way
Your main event. Crystal clear water, hidden bays, snorkelling. Go on multiple days — different islands each time. Palmižana is most popular with beach + restaurants. Stipanska is quieter. Bring snorkels, water shoes, lunch supplies. This is what Hvar is for — don't overthink it, just go.
Spanish Fortress ~€10pp / R200pp
20-minute walk up from the harbour. Go at sunset — views over the Pakleni Islands, harbour, and old town rooftops are extraordinary. Worth every cent.
Dubovica Beach Free
8km from Hvar Town, stunning sheltered cove, one of the most beautiful on the island. Taxi or scooter rental to get there.
Stiniva Cove Free
Dramatic narrow cliff entrance opening to a perfect pebble bay. One of Croatia's most photographed beaches. Further away — worth it for one day trip.
Hula Hula Beach Cocktails · €€
The famous Hvar sunset bar-beach. Gets going from 4pm. Not a natural beach but a great social scene, cocktails, music, right on the water.
Lavender fields Free
Mid-July is peak bloom — perfect timing. Take local bus or scooter toward Stari Grad — you'll see them along the road. Free and beautiful.
Stari Grad Free · half day
Local bus from Hvar Town. UNESCO listed old town, ancient Greek grid streets, stone houses, vines. Very quiet compared to Hvar Town. Good for one slow morning.
Rent a scooter ~€30–40 / R600–800 per day
One of the best things you can do on Hvar. Find your own beach, stop at a lavender field, have lunch in Jelsa. Complete freedom.

Where to eat in Hvar

Coffee at cafés behind the harbour Riva — avoid the front row which charges tourist prices. Coffee should be €1.50–2, not €4.

Konoba Menego Lunch · ~€12–15pp
Groda neighbourhood, up the steps from main square. Family-run, traditional Dalmatian food — cold meats, cheese, local wine, grilled fish. Very popular — go early or book ahead.
Konoba Luviji Lunch · €€
Slightly off the main drag, local crowd. Simple grilled fish, pašticada (slow-braised beef), local wine. Unpretentious and excellent.
Dalmatino Dinner · ~€20–25pp
Sveti Stjepana 15, just off main square. Consistently rated one of the best in Hvar. Black risotto, grilled seafood, local lamb. Book ahead — fills fast in July.
Fig Dinner · ~€18–22pp
Small, creative menu, good wine list. Popular with a slightly younger crowd. Book ahead.
Giaxa Dinner · €€
Well-regarded option in the old town. Traditional with a modern touch. Book ahead in July.
Pakleni Islands konoba Lunch · ~€20–25pp
Small konobas on Palmižana or Jerolim islands. Fresh grilled fish, salad, local wine. More expensive but you're on a tiny Croatian island — worth it once.
  • PekaIf you see it on any menu, order it. Slow-cooked lamb or octopus under a bell-shaped lid buried in embers. Often needs ordering in advance — ask your restaurant the day before.
  • Burek from a pekaraCheap breakfast or lunch from a local bakery, €2–3.
  • Supermarket picnicLocal cheese, prosciutto, olives, bread, bottle of local wine. Eat on a beach or the harbour wall. Costs €10–12 for two — honestly one of the best meals of the trip.

Drinks in Hvar

  • Kiva BarSmall, old town, one of the most local-feeling bars in Hvar. Live music some nights. Cold beer, good atmosphere, not trying too hard.
  • Carpe DiemThe famous Hvar nightlife bar on the harbour. If you want a late one, this is where it happens.
  • Local winePlavac Mali (red) and Pošip (white) — both grown on Hvar. Order the house wine anywhere and it'll be one of these. Excellent and cheap.

Money-saving moves

  • Free beaches everywhereCroatia's best beaches are free. Skip the paid sun-lounger setups and bring a towel to the rocky coves — they're usually more beautiful anyway.
  • Dubrovnik CardIf you plan to do the walls, museums, and bus rides, the Dubrovnik Card (~€35 for 1 day) bundles them together and saves money.
